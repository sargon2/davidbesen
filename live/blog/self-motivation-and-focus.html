Self-Motivation and Focus

David Besen, 07/18/2026. Written without AI.

This morning, my dear mother asked me an important question. Her cousin just retired, and is faced with a new problem: how to stay organized and focused without external pressures. I am personally far more motivated without direct pressure from others, and in fact I find when I am told what to do, my morale and my motivation falters. Following is a bit on what my mindset is like with respect to being a self-starter. I will cover a few disparate topics that I find central.

Discovery & Analysis

I'm going to open with a technique that has application even outside of this domain. I find it's useful in a huge variety of areas large and small, such as deciding where to go for dinner, or what political ideologies to support, or for clarifying your thoughts on something you're unfamiliar with. It's called "Discovery & Analysis". I didn't invent this technique; it was taught to me years ago in an on-the-job training course.

The core idea behind discovery & analysis is simple. If you try to think comprehensively about something, that usually involves listing its areas or properties. But when you're making a list, your brain has a tendency to evaluate each item as it comes up.

For example, if I was tasked with coming up with ideas for what to provide on a snack tray for an event, my default thought process might go like this:

  • I should probably provide some fruit. But some people don't like fruit! Maybe I shouldn't provide fruit.
  • Well, maybe crackers would be good. But, a snack tray is pretty dry if it just has crackers.
  • Cheese goes well with crackers. But lots of people don't like fancy cheese. Maybe I should stick with a prepared snack tray instead.

This process is very discouraging for coming up with new ideas. It feels like every idea you come up with gets shot down. This is the problem discovery & analysis targets. To use discovery & analysis, we simply come up with as many ideas as we can, and write them down as soon as we come up with them, regardless of how applicable or relevant or good the ideas seem to be. This really helps the creativity flow.

For our snack tray example, it might look like this:

  • Snacks
  • Fruit
  • Crackers
  • Cheese
  • Protein
  • Meat
  • Healthy foods
  • Unhealthy foods
  • Fun foods
  • Gross foods
  • Stuff that keeps well when left out
  • Doritos
  • Peanut butter and celery
  • Chicken wings
  • Lobster
  • An entire roasted pig
  • Cashew nuts
  • Ice cream
  • ...

Then, only after we come up with that list do we start evaluating. Clearly some ideas are better than others on the list, and when we can see all the ideas together, we're in a much better position to evaluate that. If we just delay that evaluation step, it gives our brain room to be creative and come up with really good ideas along with the bad ones. If you try to shortcut the process to avoid listing everything and evaluate early, the creative part of your brain immediately shuts down.

Written succinctly, the process is:

  1. Come up with and write down as many ideas as you can, regardless of how good they are.
  2. Once you can't come up with any more ideas, analyze the ideas for their quality. Expand on them, reorder them, tweak them. Make them good.

This is why the process is called discovery & analysisNote that this technique is especially useful when done as a group, because the group "list everything" energy can be quite fun and powerful. When doing it as a group, some people will naturally speak up to badmouth ideas. If/when that happens, simply continue writing down the idea despite the complaint. That way all of the ideas get captured, and when you don't write down the complaint it doesn't validate it, allowing everyone to continue coming up with ideas.. It's always these two steps. Its simplicity is part of what makes it so applicable to so many situations. It's truly a valuable tool and I turn to it first when presented with all sorts of different categories of problem.

Internal Locus of Control

The locus of control is the extent to which someone believes that they, as opposed to uncontrollable external forces in their life, determine the outcomes from events and activities in their life. Building and maintaining an internal locus of control, as opposed to the default belief that everything happens to you, is a long, difficult task that I am not expert enough on to cover here. There are lots of resources online about this and it's absolutely worth pursuing.

I would phrase this as: You need to fully convince yourself that the things you do and the way you do them directly affect the outcomes in your life. It's not enough to tell yourself that this is true, you have to genuinely believe it, deeply and to the point that it automatically and thoughtlessly motivates you to do things without the use of willpower.

Extrinsic vs. Intrinsic Motivators

Extrinsic motivation is when you do things for a reward. Intrinsic is when you do things for the sake of doing them. Examples of extrinsic motivators are money, grades, food, bonuses, and the desire to avoid punishment. Examples of intrinsic motivators are curiosity, enjoyment, personal growth, and flow.

Both external and internal motivators can get you to do things. But, external motivators are fickle, precocious, and unreliable. Intrinsic motivators are much more steady and personal.

The main drivers of intrinsic motivation are autonomy, mastery, purpose, and curiosity. If you don't have enough intrinsic motivation, it's because you're missing one or more of those things.

Self-Discipline

To many, discipline and self-discipline are something punishing that must be avoided or hated or minimized. But, at its core, discipline is deceptively simple, and even useful. It doesn't have to feel bad to implement.

In our life, we have desires. We can categorize these desires into two categories: generally short-term desires, such as wanting candy or not wanting to get up from the couch, and generally long-term desires, such as wanting to get in shape or to own a mansion. Discipline is prioritization of the long-term over the short-term desires when the two conflict.

That's it. That's all discipline is. It's saying you would rather get in shape than eat ice cream, because getting in shape is the longer term goal. It's not about hurting yourself, or suffering, or anything bad. It's just taking two different goals you have, and consciously, deliberately, and mindfully prioritizing them. It's about just remembering your long-term goal when a short-term goal is right in front of you. Discipline is memory.

Discovering and Living with Goals

You should have goals that you genuinely believe in. These are areas where your life or the world doesn't live up to your ideal, and your ideal is reasonable and achievable. If you don't have these, or don't have ones you like or believe in, apply discovery & analysis to discover and rank them by importance. This list of goals is your main source for work that needs to be done.

Usually goals, when stated, aren't really that actionable, even if they're achievable. In order to be actionable, they must be broken down into smaller pieces. If you break down a goal enough, it becomes something you can actually do. Also, it's good to include reasons behind your goals in the form of "so that" statements, such as "I would like to do X so that Y happens," but that's not a hard requirement.

For example, let's say your goal is "Spend more time in nature to support my physical and mental health". Then your top-level breakdown might be to first decide when to do it, then decide where & how to do it. That gives you two tasks. Then you can break down the 2nd task into smaller steps such as "Perform discovery & analysis to decide what to do in nature" and "Go buy hiking shoes." I find when I don't feel like doing a task, often the problem is just that it isn't broken down enough, and when I break it down further it becomes appealing to do.

Almost all goals roll up to two large goal categories: career, and relationships. Then general self-improvement goals are usually in service of one or both of those two goals. When you discover something you want to do or to happen, it helps to ask yourself why you want that. You'll find it usually (but not always) rolls up to one of those two top-level categories.

Scheduling

Scheduling is important for organizing the things you do. There are only a few hours in the day, and there's more that you want to complete than you have time for. So, you have to prioritize things. Unexpected things happen during the day, so the more important something is, the earlier in the day it should go. Then when something unexpected happens, it's easier to reschedule.

It's a big mistake to treat your schedule as your boss. It's just a tool to decide what time to do things, not a way to force yourself to do things. If it comes time to do something according to your schedule, and you aren't going to do it, then reschedule or delete it.

I just use Google Calendar for all my scheduling needs. I think any tool is probably good enough.

Flow State

Entire books have been written on flow. But, the top-level overview is that some tasks are too easy, so you find them boring. Some tasks are too hard, so you find them frustrating. But between those two extremes, there are tasks that are just right to work on, and it becomes very pleasant to work on them uninterrupted for a period of time. It's the goal of any work session to enter & stay in the flow state.

Heads-Down Time

I like to schedule some "heads-down" time pretty frequently. This is time where you don't use distractions and don't talk to others. It's time for you to get into the flow state and do some work. I use a Pomodoro-like schedule, with regular scheduled breaks.

Rituals for Repetitive Tasks

Some tasks must be repeated frequently and for a long time. For example, brushing your teeth isn't an intellectually interesting task, but must be done all the time. For these kind of tasks, I find it helps to think of them as rituals. If you control the environment you do the tasks in, by (for example) adjusting the lighting, music, and mood, it makes these tasks a lot easier and more enjoyable to do over and overSome people say you should also adjust the smell of your task area as well, but I personally don't like artificial smells..

Second Brain

As humans, our memory is imperfect. Computers and pen/paper are pretty good at remembering things. So, we should use tools to aid our memory and to provide space to work through things. This can be software, or a physical journal, or whatever works for you. I organize my notes based on topic, and use it to remember everything -- recipes, lists of activities or events, ideas for things to work on, etc.

I like to use Workflowy as my 2nd brain, along with a few other tools, and I've used other software in the past. I like Workflowy because it allows for hierarchical breakdown, and that aligns well with how I think. Anything is fine as long as it's easy for you to access and you trust it.

Conclusion

These are only some of the more high-priority tools & ideas that I use to help self-motivate to live my life the way I want to. There are many more. Of course different ways to live one's life appeal to different people, and some of these ideas may not be useful for you. Of course that's fine.

When I look around, I see a lot of people that genuinely hate going to their jobs every day. I think that's a travesty. It's so unfortunate that the way our society is set up forces people to do work they don't want to do every day. And I hope we can find a path forward from that that works for everyone, not just for some. 🙨
